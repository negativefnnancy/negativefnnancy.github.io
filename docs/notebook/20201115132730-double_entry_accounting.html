Concepts

It describes double entry accounting.

Apparently, credit and debit have specific meanings in accounting: a credit is when you add money to an account in question, and a debit is when you subtract money from the account! I think this is cool hehe.

An account is defined as simply a place where money goes. This doesn’t have to correspond to someone’s real world bank account. You could define an account, for example, to hold your grocery expenses. Such an abstract account doesn’t correspond to any real world location for holding money, but it is symbolic of all the money you spend on groceries.

A journal is a record of all transactions.

A transaction is when money moves from a source account to a target account. For every debit from one account, there is a corresponding credit to another account.

At the end of the day, all credits and debits must sum to a total of 0. If this is the case, then your accounts have been balanced, and all the money has been accounted for! XD

Double entry accounting is maintaining a journal such that each entry explicitly accounts for both the source and target accounts of every transaction.

Examples

Income example

Here’s an example entry from the manual: 

9/29  My Employer
    Assets:Checking                           $500.00
    Income:Salary                            $-500.00

In this example, there are two accounts: Assets:Checking (the user’s checking account) and Income:Salary (an abstract account representing where the salary comes from).

The example illustrates a transaction such that money moves from the employer (salary account) to the user’s checking account.

Thus there is a debit (subtraction) from the salary account (the source account) and a credit (addition) to the checking account (the target account).

All credits and debits (in this case just one of each) sum to 0, so we know we have accounted for all the money!

Expenses example

Here’s another example entry from the manual: 

2010/12/20 * Organic Co-op
    Expenses:Food:Groceries             $ 37.50  ; [=2011/01/01]
    Expenses:Food:Groceries             $ 37.50  ; [=2011/02/01]
    Expenses:Food:Groceries             $ 37.50  ; [=2011/03/01]
    Expenses:Food:Groceries             $ 37.50  ; [=2011/04/01]
    Expenses:Food:Groceries             $ 37.50  ; [=2011/05/01]
    Expenses:Food:Groceries             $ 37.50  ; [=2011/06/01]
    Assets:Checking                   $ -225.00

In this example, there are also two accounts: Expenses:Food:Groceries (an abstract account representing grocery expenses) and Assets:Checking (again the user’s checking account).

This example illustrates a series of transactions such that money moves from the user’s checking account to the grocery store, i.e., it’s a series of payments towards groceries.

There are a series of credits to the target account, one for the first of each month from January to June (inclusive). And a single debit is expressed from the source account which corresponds to the total of all the credits.

So once again, all the credits and debits sum to 0!
