Between Ubiquity and Perfection

There is a natural tension for me (and probably for others) between striking a balance between finding compatibility with the rest of the world and with seeking perfection. Often times the world just isn’t perfect and doing things the way others do them is always going to be unsatisfying. But it can also be really uncomforting to deviate from the status quo because of the introduced complexity in maintaining an large functioning systems on your own without knowing you can fall back onto the shoulders of whats already in place. Even worse is partially deviating from that status quo to satisfy a curiosity about the greener grass elsewhere but without actually getting there. Then you’re stuck without the satisfaction of conforming to a ubiquitous system and without the satisfaction of having found perfection. That’s how it is for me!

In general, the further off you veer from the status quo, the more work you have to put in to remain functioning. For example, most people use Windows. If you use Windows, you get the luxury of support and the ability to talk to nearly anybody and take part in the Windows-world things that all your friends do. You might even get a certain warm fuzzy feeling of doing things the standard way. If you use, say Linux, you suddenly dip into a world where you can make things so much better than they can be in Windows! But you sacrifice a bit of communication along the way. Suddenly playing Windows games with your friends for example becomes slightly more of an battle than it otherwise would be. Not to mention you generally have to take much more responsibility for your system. (But that’s why you wanted too use Linux to begin with right?) But Linux isn’t so bad, it’s actually still very ubiquitous and many distros are very well supported. On the more extreme end however, say, using an operating system like OpenBSD, a very opinionated and niche operating system! If you like these opinions, using this operating system might really tingly your perfection seeking sense! But forget all the conveniences you get with Linux! It’s a very small community that supports OpenBSD by comparison and you don’t have nearly as much to stand on. You’ll have to put in so much more work to find support for your specific needs.

Another example is messaging platforms. Skype used to be where all my friends were. But Skype is awful! I hated it and I wanted to use something else. But if I did that I wouldn’t be able to talk to my friends. Oh well! Luckily we have Discord now which is better, but it’s still not great. I’d rather explore more interesting platforms like Matrix. Unfortunately my friends largely aren’t there and using bridging software is just extremely finnicky and imperfect.

There seem to be times when I favor ubiquity and times when I favor perfection. And I always seem to be striking a compromise!

Some clear times when I favor ubiquity are when it comes to friends. In fact, I see socialization as the perfect opportunity to let my perfection senses turn off, because socialization to me is largely about the other. Perfection to me is inherently about the self. Working on group projects is a time when I’m focused on the goal of the group, which is separate from the goal I want for myself. I think making this distinction clear is very useful, because it single handedly seems to dispell any of the usual tensions and disagreements that might usually arise among group projects! When a group’s goal differs from my own goals, that’s okay! The point of the group project is to build something together rather than to satisfy my own needs.

Other times I seem to favor ubiquity are when it comes to software, language, and tooling standards.

For example, I use Arch Linux, which in my opinion has lots of flaws. But it’s certainly a lot better for me than Ubuntu or Debian or anything else. I like it because it’s minimal, has simple package management and doesn’t break very often at all. It stays out of my way and lets me configure my system the way I want it to be (which is fairly minimal itself). On the surface this seems more like a case of compromise than ubiquity. But the way I see it is that because I do use Arch Linux, it gives my brain an excuse to find pleasure in conformity to the Arch “way”.

Actually, now that I think about it the reason deviation without perfection is worse than full conformity, is because full conformity is a kind of perfection seeking! It’s perfection seeking under somebody else’s framework! The times when this is good, is when my own framework isn’t quite ready to spread its wings and fly.

And I often do find myself seeking to perfect my conformance to other people’s guidelines before branching away and establishing my own way. I think there is a lot of general value in this because puts me in a really good position to really learn from others such that it really informs my own systems!
